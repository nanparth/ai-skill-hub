# Interview Design Reference

Operational reference for designing exploratory interview scripts.

## Core Principles

1. **Elicit, never lead.**  No question should assume the answer, introduce concepts the interviewee has not mentioned, or frame the situation favourably for your hypothesis.
2. **Assume nothing.**  Audit every question: "What must be true about the interviewee for this question to make sense?"  If the answer goes beyond basic demographics, the question is too narrow for early sections.
3. **Waterfall broad to narrow.**  Context, then problems, then solutions, then value, then concept.  Each layer earns the right to ask the next.
4. **Plain language.**  No business jargon.  Use everyday analogies for pricing and service models.  Domain terminology is acceptable when interviewing professionals in that domain.
5. **Every question maps to a deliverable.**  No orphan questions.  If a question does not serve any hypothesis or application field, cut it.
6. **Concept reaction is late and time-permitting.**  Unprimed views on problems and current solutions are more valuable than reactions to a pitch.

## Script Architecture (9 Components)

1. **Basic Information Block**  -- Interviewer Info table, pre-filled where possible.  Adapt fields to the interviewee segment (role, experience, organisation type for professionals; demographics, occupation, skill level for consumers).
2. **Introduction and Consent**  -- Frame as exploratory, promise confidentiality, set duration expectation.  Three sentences maximum.  End with: "Do you have any questions for me before we begin?"
3. **Free Association Exercise**  -- Warm-up prompt (trivial, relatable) followed by 2-4 real prompts.  Prompts are situations or phrases, never questions.  Move from broad to narrow across prompts.  For supply-side or professional interviews, prompts must not assume the interviewee endorses the model or wants to participate.
4. **Background and Profile**  -- Broadest question about daily life in the relevant domain, self-assessed skill or experience level, tools or services relied on.
5. **Problem Exploration**  -- Minimum 3 questions: recent experience, specific story ("walk me through what happened"), decision and action taken.  Probe for timeline, emotional state, stakes, uncertainty, and avoidance.
6. **Current Solutions**  -- Where they go for help or information now.  Walk through first, second, third choice.  What makes sources trustworthy vs. confusing.
7. **Value and Willingness to Pay**  -- Start with past behaviour ("Have you ever paid for..."), not hypotheticals ("Would you pay for...").  Use everyday analogies for pricing frames.  Let them name the price before probing specific structures.
8. **Concept Reaction (time-permitting)**  -- Brief plain-language description of the concept.  "What is your first reaction?"  Then probe: what sounds useful, what sounds worrying, what would be a deal-breaker.
9. **Close**  -- "Is there anything I did not ask that you think is important?" plus follow-up willingness.

## Question Format

Each question follows the main question + sub-questions pattern:

- **Main question:** open-ended, stands alone, answerable without preamble.
- **Sub-questions (3-5):** conditional follow-ups, used only if the main answer does not naturally cover the ground.
- Mark redundant sub-questions with *(skip if already covered)*.
- When the interviewee pool spans distinct roles, add profile-specific skip notes so interviewers know which follow-ups apply to whom (e.g., *(skip for [role A])*, *(ask [role B] only)*).
- Include a **"Why important"** annotation per question: one sentence explaining what hypothesis or deliverable field the question serves.  This is for the interviewer, not the interviewee.

## Free Association Design Rules

- Always include a trivial warm-up ("Waiting in line," "Monday morning," "Summer vacation").
- 2-4 real prompts, each probing a different dimension of the problem space.
- Prompts are situations or phrases, never questions.
- Move from broad to narrow across prompts.
- For supply-side or professional interviews: do not presuppose the interviewee has experienced the problem, endorses the solution, or wants to participate in the platform.

## Key Points Summary Template

Include at the top of every interview result file.  Fill immediately after the interview while memories are fresh.

- **Top themes** (3-4 bullets)
- **[Bucket per hypothesis category]** (2 bullets each; adapt buckets to the specific script's hypothesis set)
- **Concept reactions** (if time-permitting section reached) (2-3 bullets)
- **Anything surprising / contradictory / worth probing in future interviews** (2-3 bullets)

## Anti-Pattern Checklist

- [ ] No leading questions (assumes the answer or frames favourably)
- [ ] No hidden assumptions about the interviewee's situation or willingness
- [ ] No business or marketing jargon (domain terminology acceptable for professional segments)
- [ ] Concept reaction does not appear before Section 7+
- [ ] No more than 15 core questions (depth beats breadth; target 45-60 min)
- [ ] No empty follow-up labels in result files ("Follow-up answers:" with nothing after it)
- [ ] Every question maps to at least one hypothesis or deliverable field

## Two-Sided Research

When interviewing for a platform or marketplace with distinct demand and supply sides, each side gets its own script.  The scripts share architecture but probe different hypotheses.

| Dimension | Demand side (users/consumers) | Supply/stakeholder side (providers/professionals) |
|---|---|---|
| Free association prompts | Situational ("You are stuck on a problem") | Relational/systemic ("An online place where people can...") |
| Background section | Usage patterns, skill level, daily routine | Experience providing help, motivations, current methods |
| Problem exploration | Pain points as a user of the system | Frustrations as a provider, professional, or gatekeeper |
| Value and willingness to pay | Would they pay, how much, what triggers payment | What is fair compensation, minimum thresholds, referral conditions |
| Concept reaction | "Would you use this?" | "Would you join/refer/partner with this?" |
